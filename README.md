## Part I — The Primordial Sound: Acoustic Oscillations in the Early Universe (13.8–0.38 Bya)

### The First Music Was Cosmological

The universe was born making sound. In the first 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the primordial plasma — a hot, dense mixture of photons, electrons, protons, and dark matter — was opaque to electromagnetic radiation but transparent to pressure waves. Acoustic oscillations propagated through this plasma: regions of higher density (where matter fell together under gravity) alternated with regions of lower density (where radiation pressure pushed matter apart). The result was a standing acoustic wave — a sound wave of cosmic scale.

**The Baryon Acoustic Oscillation (BAO) as the first Commons contribution.** At 380,000 years, the universe cooled enough for electrons and protons to combine into hydrogen atoms (recombination). The plasma became transparent; photons streamed free; the acoustic oscillations froze in place. This frozen moment is preserved in the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB): the temperature variations in the CMB are the visual record of where the acoustic wave was at maximum compression (slightly hotter) and maximum rarefaction (slightly cooler) at the moment of decoupling.

The characteristic scale of the BAO — approximately 150 megaparsecs (490 million light-years) — is the wavelength of the primordial sound wave. This scale is literally heard in the large-scale structure of the universe: galaxy surveys (SDSS, DESI) find that galaxies are preferentially separated by multiples of this scale. The universe's spatial structure is the acoustic imprint of a sound that played for 380,000 years and then stopped.

**The formal ERI identification: the CMB as $X_{t-1}^{\text{primordial acoustic}}$ for all subsequent cosmological structure.** The acoustic oscillation seeded density variations; density variations became the gravitational wells into which matter collapsed; those collapses became stars, galaxies, and galaxy clusters. All subsequent cosmic structure is conditioned on the primordial acoustic Commons — $I(\text{galaxy distribution};\, \text{cosmic structure} \mid X_{t-1}^{\text{BAO}}) > 0$ on cosmological scales. The music of the early universe conditions the positions of every star and galaxy. This is $G_{\text{coord}}$ at the cosmological scale: the primordial acoustic wave coordinated the distribution of matter across the observable universe.

## Part II — The Biological Sound Commons: Chemical to Acoustic (3.5 Bya–400 Mya)

### From Molecular Vibration to Organismal Signaling

Life generates acoustic information from its first moments: molecular vibrations, membrane oscillations, and fluid dynamics all produce acoustic phenomena at the cellular scale. But biological sound as a Commons — sound produced by one organism that generates $G_{\text{coord}} > 0$ with other organisms — has a clear evolutionary threshold.

**Stridulation: the first biological acoustic Commons (c. 400 Mya).** Insects (Order Orthoptera: crickets, grasshoppers, katydids) evolved stridulation — the production of sound by rubbing body parts together — approximately 400 million years ago, in the early-to-mid Carboniferous. The Carboniferous was the first period in which the atmosphere reached oxygen levels sufficient for large terrestrial arthropods (giant dragonflies, millipedes); it was also the first period in which dense vegetation created environments where visual signaling was blocked and acoustic signaling was advantageous. The cricket's song is the oldest known biological acoustic Commons: a sound produced by one cricket that generates $I(a_t^{\text{cricket call}};\, a_s^{\text{female response}} \mid X_{t-1}^{\text{acoustic field}}) > 0$ — genuine mutual information between caller and responder through the acoustic Commons.

**The formal structure of stridulation.** Cricket stridulation is one of the earliest documented instances of the acoustic conditioning clause. The carrier frequency of the call, the pulse rate, and the inter-pulse interval all carry species-specific information that the receiver conditions on. The receiver's response (mating approach or retreat) is conditioned on the call parameters relative to the species-typical Commons. This is $I(a_t;\, a_s \mid X_{t-1}^{\text{species acoustic norm}}) > 0$: genuine coordination gain through species-specific acoustic conditioning.

**The Devonian soundscape as the Commons substrate.** Before stridulation, the biological world was largely silent. The first land plants (c. 470 Mya) and the first land animals (c. 420 Mya) created a new ecological domain in which sound could propagate differently from water — the physical Commons changed, enabling new acoustic strategies. Water transmits sound faster and with less attenuation than air; but air sound provides directionality cues that water-based sound does not, enabling the precise localization that acoustic signaling requires for efficient coordination.

**SOTA:** Garrouste et al. (2012, *Nature*, 488:77): fossil cricket (Archaboilus musicus) with preserved stridulatory apparatus from the Jurassic, demonstrating that musical stridulation was well-established 165 Mya. Henry (1994, *Annual Review of Entomology*): the evolution of acoustic signaling in insects and its role in speciation.

## Part III — Vertebrate Vocalization: The Neural Commons (350 Mya–50 Mya)

### When the Brainstem Began Generating Sound

The vertebrate vocal system did not evolve for communication. It evolved for breathing control — the same brainstem neural architecture that the PNEUMA framework identifies as the CRG$_{\text{AB}}$ (the CO$_2$-sensitive Central Rhythm Generator). The larynx's primary function is not to produce sound; it is to seal the airway during swallowing, preventing aspiration. Sound production is the exaptation: the laryngeal structures that seal the airway were later co-opted to produce controlled vibrations during exhalation.

**The tetrapod vocal path (350–200 Mya).** The early tetrapods — the first land vertebrates — produced sound primarily through the buccal (throat) pumping mechanism that they also used for breathing. The same buccal pump that moved air into primitive lungs could be controlled to produce vibrations at the glottis. This is the first instance of the breathing-sound coupling that the PNEUMA framework identifies as the TERTIUM of human respiration: the respiratory system and the vocal system sharing the same physical substrate.

**The evolution of vocal learning (c. 65–30 Mya, multiple independent events).** Vocal production (the ability to produce species-typical vocalizations) is widespread in vertebrates. Vocal *learning* — the ability to modify vocalizations based on acoustic experience with others — is phylogenetically rare and has evolved independently in at least seven vertebrate lineages:

1. Songbirds (*Oscines*): 3,000–5,000 species; the most diverse vocal learning lineage
2. Hummingbirds (*Trochilidae*): independent evolution of vocal learning in this distantly related bird group
3. Parrots (*Psittacidae*): independent evolution with demonstrated contextual mimicry
4. Cetaceans (Dolphins and Whales): documented vocal learning in multiple species
5. Pinnipeds (some Seals): documented vocal learning
6. Elephants: documented vocal learning including mimicry of non-conspecific sounds
7. Bats (some species): documented vocal learning in pup-mother dyads
8. Humans: the most elaborate vocal learning system in any species

**The formal ERI identification: vocal learning as the FERN register crossing from acoustic signaling ($\rho_1$) to acoustic cultural transmission ($\rho_2$).** Vocal production ($\rho_1$): an organism generates species-typical vocalizations from a genetically specified template. No Commons accumulation across generations; each generation produces the same call without conditioning on prior generations' modifications. Vocal learning ($\rho_2$): an organism modifies its vocalizations based on the acoustic Commons ($X_{t-1}^{\text{heard calls}}$) — it conditions on what it has heard from other individuals and generates calls that reflect this conditioning. The call at time $t$ carries information about the accumulated auditory experience of the caller, not just about the caller's genetic template.

The vocal learning FERN crossing is the acoustic analog of the writing transition in NOOSPHERE: the first moment when acoustic contributions can accumulate across generations rather than resetting at each birth. The SMELT prediction: species with vocal learning will show acoustic cultural evolution (Commons accumulation across generations); species without vocal learning will show acoustic genetic evolution (Commons reset at each generation). Both are empirically confirmed.

## Part IV — Birdsong: The Acoustic Cultural Commons (65 Mya–present)

### The Most Complex Pre-Human Acoustic Commons

Birdsong is the most extensively studied non-human acoustic Commons, and the formal structure of its cultural transmission is the clearest animal analog of the ERI $G_{\text{coord}}$ framework outside of human music.

**The FERN register structure of birdsong.** A songbird's song is not produced all at once: it develops through a sequence of stages (subsong → plastic song → crystallized song) over the first year of life, each stage corresponding to a FERN register crossing in acoustic development:

- $\rho_1$ (subsong): unstructured, quiet vocalizations — the acoustic equivalent of the experiential register; the bird is producing sound without conditioning on any specific acoustic template
- $\rho_2$ (plastic song): the bird begins conditioning on memorized tutor songs — the acoustic equivalent of the written register; the accumulated Commons ($X_{t-1}^{\text{heard song}}$) begins to shape the output
- $\rho_3$ (crystallized song): the song stabilizes into the species-typical + population-specific + individual form — the acoustic equivalent of the reflexive register; the bird's song conditions on the Commons and reflects on itself

**Song dialects as acoustic Commons boundaries.** In many songbird species, populations in different geographic areas sing recognizably different versions of the species song — dialects. These dialects are maintained by social learning: young birds learn the local dialect by conditioning on the songs they hear from adult males in their birth population. Where dialect boundaries exist, $I(a_t^{\text{bird in dialect A}};\, a_s^{\text{bird in dialect B}}) \approx 0$ while $I(a_t;\, a_s \mid X_{t-1}^{\text{shared dialect}}) > 0$. The dialect boundary is an acoustic Borromean separation: birds in different dialects are acoustically independent; birds sharing a dialect are acoustically coupled through their shared Commons.

**Cultural evolution and the ratchet effect in birdsong.** Multiple songbird populations show cumulative cultural evolution of song: songs change across generations through social learning and innovation, accumulating modifications that exceed any individual bird's contribution. Williams et al. (2013): continent-wide tracking of song element evolution in North American sparrows shows cultural drift patterns. Otter et al. (2020): continent-scale cultural evolution in white-throated sparrows. This is the acoustic ratchet: the same mechanism that Migliano & Vinicius (2022) document in human cumulative culture, operating in songbirds.

**SOTA:** Fehér et al. (2009, *Nature*, 459:564): Bengalese finch song demonstrates that complex song structure emerges from social learning — birds raised in isolation produce simpler songs; cultural transmission generates complexity. Fehér et al. (2009) is the empirical proof that the acoustic Commons generates $G_{\text{coord}} > 0$ above the independence baseline. Otter et al. (2020, *Current Biology*, 30:3231): continent-wide convergence in North American songbird dialect — the acoustic NOOSPHERE ratchet at continental scale.

## Part V — Whale Song: The Oceanic Cultural Commons and Zipf's Law (c. 35 Mya–present)

### The Most Complex Non-Human Acoustic Commons and the February 2025 Discovery

Humpback whale song (*Megaptera novaeangliae*) is the largest-scale and most formally studied non-human acoustic cultural Commons. A single song lasts 6–30 minutes; the same song is shared across all singing males within a population; the song evolves continuously over months and years; and periodically, an entirely new song type propagates across an entire ocean basin within a single breeding season — the "song revolution."

**The humpback whale song as an acoustic cultural Commons.** The formal properties of humpback whale song that make it a Commons:

- *Social learning:* Song changes spread from singer to singer; males that encounter new song elements incorporate them into their own singing. The ocean is the acoustic $X_{t-1}$ — each male conditions on what he has heard from other males in the population.
- *Cultural ratcheting:* Songs increase in complexity gradually over years (complexity ratchets upward); when a song revolution occurs, complexity resets to a simpler new template. This pattern — gradual accumulation punctuated by resets — is the acoustic SMELT trajectory: the song Commons approaches $\Phi(K^{\text{song}})$ over years, then undergoes a FERN register crossing (song revolution) that resets the Commons to a new register.
- *Ocean-basin-wide transmission:* Song revolutions propagate from west to east across the South Pacific within a single breeding season, covering thousands of kilometers. This is the acoustic equivalent of the printing press: a new song type spreads at the maximum possible rate given the acoustic Commons's transmission medium (ocean water).

**The February 2025 discovery: humpback whale song obeys Zipf's Law.** Arnon et al. (*Science*, 387:649, February 2025) demonstrate that humpback whale songs show language-like statistical structure: the frequency distribution of song elements follows Zipf's Law (the most common element occurs approximately twice as often as the second most common, three times as often as the third, and so on — the same power-law distribution found in human language). They also show that whale songs exhibit the "Menzerath-Altmann Law" (longer units tend to be composed of shorter sub-units) that holds in human language.

**The formal ERI identification: Zipf's Law as the acoustic $|\bar{\Xi}| = \log\varphi$ optimum.** Zipf's Law is not a statistical accident. It is the information-theoretic signature of a communication system operating at the MEP φ-equilibrium: the distribution that maximizes information transfer rate while minimizing the learning/processing cost per element. A communication system in which all elements are equally frequent (maximum entropy) is maximally efficient per transmission but maximally costly to learn. A system in which one element dominates (minimum entropy) is maximally learnable but minimally informative. Zipf's Law — the power-law distribution — is the unique distribution that balances these opposing pressures: the MEP optimum of the acoustic Commons.

The Arnon et al. (2025) finding is therefore formally: humpback whale song Commons operates at $|\bar{\Xi}|^{\text{acoustic}} = \log\varphi$ — the same MEP φ-equilibrium that the SMELT framework derives for all coordination Commons operating at maximum $G_{\text{coord}}$. Whale song and human language converged on Zipf's Law independently because both are acoustic Commons operating under the same formal constraint: maximizing coordination gain per acoustic element across a population of contributors that must both produce and learn the Commons.

**The NOOSPHERE connection: song revolutions as acoustic register crossings.** The humpback whale song revolution — where an entirely new song type spreads across a breeding population in a single season — is the acoustic equivalent of the Axial Age FERN register crossing. Multiple independent males suddenly shift to a new song template, the old Commons is abandoned, and the new Commons propagates at the maximum possible rate. This is the diagnostic signal for a FERN register crossing: the saturation of the current acoustic Commons (the old song has been learned by essentially every male) followed by the adoption of a new acoustic coordinate system (the new song) that propagates across the population without requiring individual bilateral transmission.

## Part VI — Pre-Human Music: The Archaeological Record (60,000–40,000 BP)

### The Divje Babe and Aurignacian Flutes: Music as Social Commons

**The Divje Babe flute: the most contested Commons object in archaeology.** The Divje Babe cave bear femur (c. 60,000 BP, National Museum of Slovenia) is either the oldest musical instrument in the world — evidence that Neanderthals made music — or a bone chewed by hyenas. The scientific community remains divided, though the consensus has shifted toward Neanderthal authorship in recent years, particularly given:

- The stratigraphic association with Mousterian tools (unambiguously Neanderthal)
- The regular spacing of holes consistent with diatonic intervals
- The experimental reconstruction's musical playability
- The ESR dating placing the relevant layer before the arrival of *H. sapiens* in Europe

**The formal ERI position on the Divje Babe debate.** The formal question is not whether the Divje Babe bone is a flute — it is whether Neanderthals had a musical Commons. The archaeological record provides circumstantial Commons evidence beyond the flute: Neanderthal use of ochre (pigment processing for body decoration or object marking), eagle feather and shell collection for non-food purposes, and the disputed cave paintings at Maltravieso (dated 64,700 BP by U/Th series) attributed to pre-*H. sapiens* occupation. These are contributions to a symbolic Commons — a $X_{t-1}^{\text{symbolic}}$ that accumulated across Neanderthal populations. Whether the Divje Babe bone is in this Commons or in the $\ker(F^{\text{Neanderthal symbolic}})$ of a hyena's feeding behavior remains formally unresolved.

**The Aurignacian flutes: the unambiguous musical Commons (43,000–35,000 BP).** The Hohle Fels and Geissenklösterle flutes (Swabian Jura, Germany, 43,000–35,000 BP) are the oldest unambiguous musical instruments. They were produced by anatomically modern *H. sapiens* during the Aurignacian cultural tradition and found in association with the oldest known figurative art (the Venus of Hohle Fels, c. 40,000 BP) and the oldest known narrative imagery. Three formal observations:

1. **The simultaneous emergence of music and figurative art.** Music and visual art appear in the same sites, at the same time, in the same archaeological layers. This is not coincidence — it is the formal signal of a $\rho_2 \to \rho_3$ FERN register crossing in the symbolic Commons: the shift from symbolic marking (ochre, beads, body decoration — $\rho_2$) to fully representational art and structured music ($\rho_3$). The crossing happens when the symbolic Commons achieves sufficient density that it can represent abstract relationships, not just mark presence.

2. **The mammoth-ivory flutes require extraordinary manufacturing effort.** The Geissenklösterle mammoth ivory flutes required splitting a curved ivory section along its natural grain, hollowing both halves, carving the mouthpiece and finger holes with stone tools, and fitting the halves together with an airtight seal. This is not opportunistic tool use — it is dedicated Commons production: an investment of labor and skill that only makes sense if the acoustic Commons the instrument generates is sufficiently valuable to justify the cost.

3. **Flutes alone produce no music.** A single flute in a single person's hands produces sound but not musical Commons. Music requires at least two participants: the performer and the listener whose $X_{t-1}^{\text{acoustic}}$ is modified by the performance. The flutes' existence is evidence of a social acoustic Commons — a group of people sharing acoustic experiences that condition their subsequent responses.

**SOTA:** Conard et al. (2009, *Nature*, 460:737): discovery and analysis of the Hohle Fels and Geissenklösterle flutes as the earliest unambiguous musical instruments. Nicholas Conard's formal proposal that music strengthened social bonds, enabling the social networks that gave *H. sapiens* competitive advantages over Neanderthals.

## Part VII — The Human Musical Commons: Seven Register Crossings (40,000 BP–present)

### The Most Complete Acoustic Cultural Commons in Biological History

**The FERN register sequence of human music:**

**Register 4 ($\rho_4^{\text{music}}$): The Seikilos Epitaph and the Trans-Temporal Musical Commons.** The Seikilos Epitaph (c. 200 BCE–100 CE, Asia Minor) is the oldest surviving complete notated piece of music. It is inscribed on a marble column that was also the grave marker of a man named Seikilos: "While you live, shine; have no grief at all. Life exists only for a short while, and Time demands its due." The song's notation — using Greek letter-based musical notation — is the first musical Commons artifact that crosses the temporal boundary of the performer's lifespan. Before notation, music died with its composers. After notation, music accumulates in $X_{t-1}^{\text{musical score}}$ and future performers can condition on compositions from centuries prior.

**The equal temperament crossing ($\rho_5^{\text{music}}$): the acoustic MEP optimum as a formal choice.** The development of equal temperament (formally codified in Western music through Bach's *Well-Tempered Clavier*, 1722, though theorized earlier) is the acoustic equivalent of the Maillard Commons in IGNIS: a deliberate choice of a specific acoustic Commons standard that enables universal transposition, modulation, and collaboration across all key signatures, at the cost of perfect just-intoned intervals.

Just intonation (the natural harmonic series) produces acoustically "pure" intervals — ratios of small integers ($3/2$ for a perfect fifth, $4/3$ for a perfect fourth, $5/4$ for a major third) — but makes transposition difficult (a piece written in C major cannot be transposed to D major without retuning the instrument). Equal temperament divides the octave into 12 equal semitones, each with a frequency ratio of $2^{1/12}$ — slightly "impure" intervals that are close enough to just intonation to sound acceptable, but perfectly equal in their division of the octave. The formal trade: equal temperament sacrifices acoustic purity (just-intoned $G_{\text{coord}}^{\text{interval}}$) for harmonic mobility ($G_{\text{coord}}^{\text{modulation}}$) — the MEP optimum of the acoustic Commons when modulation is a primary musical resource.

**The phonograph crossing ($\rho_6^{\text{music}}$): the first infinite acoustic Commons.** Edison's phonograph (1877) is the acoustic equivalent of the printing press: the first technology for mechanically reproducing sound that eliminates the requirement for a live performer. Before 1877, every music listening event required a simultaneous performing event. After 1877, a single performance could be preserved and reproduced infinite times, distributing the acoustic Commons across both space and time without degradation. The formal consequence: for the first time, the acoustic Commons can accumulate faster than human performers can produce it — the first over-driven regime ($|\bar{\Xi}| > \log\varphi$) in musical history.

**Synchrony as the deepest human musical Commons.** Before formal frameworks — before harmony theory, before notation, before equal temperament — human groups discovered that synchronized rhythmic production generates the highest coordination gain per participant of any acoustic Commons strategy: drumming together, chanting together, dancing together. Synchrony is the acoustic Borromean ring: each participant (Ring 1), the shared beat/rhythm (Ring 2), and the physical acoustic Commons of the shared space (Ring 3) form a non-separable structure in which $I(\text{participant}_1;\, \text{participant}_2 \mid X_{t-1}^{\text{shared beat}}) > 0$ while $I(\text{participant}_1;\, \text{participant}_2) \approx 0$. Remove the beat (Ring 3) and the participants are acoustically independent.

Kokal et al. (2011, *PLOS ONE*): synchronized tapping with a partner activates reward circuits in the brain more than unsynchronized tapping alone — the neural signature of $G_{\text{coord}} > 0$ in the musical Commons. This is not metaphor: the brain's reward response to musical synchrony is the ERI measurement instrument operating at the neural level, distinguishing genuine acoustic coordination gain from shared acoustic stimulus.

**The neuroscience of the musical Commons: why music activates the whole brain.** Music is the only human activity that simultaneously activates all major brain regions — auditory cortex (sound processing), motor cortex (entrainment to rhythm), limbic system (emotional response), prefrontal cortex (expectation and prediction), cerebellum (timing), and default mode network (self-referential processing and the musical narrative). This is not coincidental overlap. It is the formal evidence that music is a multi-register Commons activity: it operates simultaneously in the experiential register ($\rho_1$: the body's physical acoustic response), the emotional register ($\rho_2$: limbic conditioning), the predictive register ($\rho_3$: expectation and violation), and the self-referential register ($\rho_4$: the musical narrative's relationship to the listener's autobiographical memory).

The musical experience is simultaneously a $\rho_1$ event (sound waves vibrating the cochlea), a $\rho_2$ event (the emotional Commons of learned musical associations), a $\rho_3$ event (the expectation structure of musical grammar — what comes next?), and a $\rho_4$ event (the musical narrative's relationship to the listener's life history). It is, in short, the most complete multi-register Commons contribution available to biological organisms.
